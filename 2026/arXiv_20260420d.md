## Quasars, CGM, and AGN feedback

- [2604.16294](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16294) Nicolas Ledos et al. `The hydrodynamical response of cold circumgalactic clouds to quasar radiation`
  - **Question:** how does quasar ionizing radiation change the structure, survival, and Lyα output of cold `10^4 K` circumgalactic clouds?
  - **Data:**
    - Analytical cloud-response framework: optically thin, radiation-shielded, and rocket-effect regimes
    - Radiation-hydrodynamic simulations: static cold CGM clouds exposed to quasar EUV radiation fields
    - Lyα-emission calculations: decomposition into recombination and fluorescent contributions
  - **Method:**
    - Solve for the ionization and dynamical response of cold clouds under different incident quasar fluxes
    - Classify the resulting behavior into optically thin, shielded, and rocket-effect regimes
    - Track how hydrodynamical expansion and acceleration modify the emitted Lyα luminosity
    - Compare bright- and faint-quasar cases using Lyman-limit specific luminosities `L_nu,LL`
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The cloud response is not purely radiative-transfer limited; hydrodynamics changes the observable Lyα output once the radiation field is strong enough to drive a rocket effect.
    - In the rocket-effect regime, Lyα can become up to `~10x` brighter than in the optically thin case and can still reach `~70%` of the luminosity expected from pure fluorescence without hydrodynamical response.
    - Unless a cloud remains strongly shielded, roughly `~50-60%` of the Lyα luminosity comes from recombination rather than from simple fluorescent reprocessing.
    - Bright quasars with `L_nu,LL ~ 10^31.6 erg s^-1 Hz^-1` likely ionize most cold CGM clouds completely, while faint quasars with `L_nu,LL ~ 10^28.6 erg s^-1 Hz^-1` are more likely to leave clouds in the rocket-effect regime.
  - **Position:** a useful theory-side bridge between quasar-illuminated Lyα-nebula work and cloud-survival studies, especially if you want to interpret extended Lyα around quasars as a coupled radiation-plus-hydrodynamics problem rather than as pure fluorescence alone

- [2604.15438](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15438) Mallory D. Thorp et al. `Surveying the Whirlpool at Arcseconds with NOEMA (SWAN). IV. Extent of active galactic nucleus feedback on the interstellar medium`
  - **Question:** over what spatial extent does the low-luminosity Seyfert nucleus in M51 alter dense gas and ionized-gas conditions in the surrounding ISM?
  - **Data:**
    - VENGA IFU: optical emission-line maps used to separate AGN-dominated and star-formation-dominated regions
    - NOEMA SWAN survey: HCN, HNC, HCO+, and N2H+ at `~180 pc` resolution
    - Multiphase outflow context: radio jet plus soft X-ray field from the active nucleus
  - **Method:**
    - Define AGN-dominated zones with an emission-line-ratio function (`ELR`)
    - Compare dense-gas line ratios inside and outside the AGN-dominated footprint
    - Test whether molecular-line ratios alone recover the same feedback extent as the optical-line-ratio map
    - Interpret the gas response in terms of mechanical jet interaction plus radiative excitation
  - **Conclusion:**
    - AGN-dominated regions show elevated HCN, HNC, and HCO+ relative to N2H+, which means the nucleus changes dense-gas chemistry and excitation beyond the unresolved core.
    - The optical `ELR > 0.5` boundary traces the AGN-affected footprint more cleanly than the molecular ratios by themselves.
    - The molecular and ionized gas are not explained by a single feedback channel, because the pattern is more consistent with a two-stage process: mechanical jet-ISM interaction first and soft-X-ray molecular excitation second.
    - Outside the AGN-dominated zone, the disk remains primarily star-formation dominated, so the feedback imprint is extended but not galaxy-wide.
  - **Position:** mainly a local calibration paper, but useful if you need an observational template for separating AGN-driven versus star-formation-driven gas diagnostics in multiphase feedback studies

## LRDs, compact red populations, and BH interpretation

- [2604.16178](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16178) Euclid Collaboration et al. `Euclid: Scaled-up little red dots and other sources with v-shaped spectral energy distributions at z>4`
  - **Question:** do wide-area Euclid data reveal a brighter and more massive extension of the `z>4` little-red-dot and little-blue-dot population seen in JWST deep fields?
  - **Data:**
    - Euclid near-infrared imaging over `0.75 deg^2` in COSMOS
    - Spitzer/IRAC photometry: longer-wavelength anchor for the `V`-shaped SED selection
    - Morphology and compactness measurements: separation of compact candidates from the broader `V`-shaped population
    - AGN catalog cross-matches: identification of already known active nuclei
  - **Method:**
    - Select `z>4` sources with `V`-shaped SEDs from Euclid plus IRAC photometry
    - Measure compactness relative to the population median and isolate the most compact outliers
    - Fit stellar-population properties and compare them with published JWST LRD/LBD samples
    - Test how stricter redness cuts change the final compact-candidate sample
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The survey identifies `233` `V`-shaped `z>4` sources, but only `16` are compact at more than `1 sigma` above the median compactness and therefore qualify as robust LRD/LBD-like candidates.
    - These candidates are much more massive than most JWST deep-field LRDs, with stellar masses `10^8.5-10^10.5 Msun`, which means Euclid is picking out a scaled-up bright end of the same broad phenomenology.
    - Roughly half of the selected systems are inferred to be as old as the Universe at their redshift, which makes pure very-young-burst interpretations difficult for a substantial fraction of the sample.
    - Fewer than `10%` of all `V`-shaped `z>4` sources are already known AGN, and only `2/16` of the final compact candidates satisfy the strictest `beta_red > 0` cut, so the recovered population depends strongly on how narrowly the redness criterion is defined.
  - **Position:** an important bridge from JWST small-area LRD work to wide-area Euclid demographics, especially for testing which parts of the compact-red population are rare AGN-like systems and which are simply the bright extended tail of a broader class

- [2604.15743](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15743) Yongyun Chen et al. `A universal relationship between the variability timescale and black hole mass in black hole jetted and non-jetted accreting systems`
  - **Question:** is there a single variability-timescale versus BH-mass relation that holds across jetted and non-jetted accreting BH systems?
  - **Data:**
    - ZTF DR23 optical light curves: damped-random-walk fits for AGN variability
    - BAT-selected comparison set: `125` newly identified non-jetted AGN added to the relation
    - Multi-class BH sample: jetted and non-jetted systems spanning a broad BH-mass baseline
  - **Method:**
    - Fit characteristic optical variability timescales with a damped-random-walk model
    - Compare the scaling relation separately for jetted and non-jetted AGN
    - Test whether the inferred slope remains consistent across the full BH-mass baseline
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The characteristic variability timescale scales with BH mass in a nearly universal way, with fitted slopes of roughly `~0.35-0.50`.
    - The relation remains consistent across jetted and non-jetted systems, which argues that jet production does not strongly change the underlying mass-timescale scaling.
    - Adding `125` BAT-selected non-jetted AGN materially strengthens the low-scatter empirical baseline.
    - The result favors a common accretion-timescale origin across BH classes rather than separate variability laws for jet-dominated and jet-quiet systems.
  - **Position:** not a direct high-z result, but a compact interpretation paper that may be useful when judging whether unusual compact-red sources should or should not follow ordinary AGN variability expectations

## Metallicity diagnostics and high-z methods

- [2604.16273](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16273) F. Fabián Rosales-Ortega et al. `The DESIRED strong-line calibrations: I. New empirical metallicity relations for the local and high-redshift universe`
  - **Question:** do current strong-line metallicity diagnostics need separate high-redshift recalibration, or can a single empirical framework span both local and `z>2` galaxy samples?
  - **Data:**
    - Calibration compilation: `2392` spectra from `201` literature references
    - Extragalactic H II regions: `1029` spectra
    - Local star-forming galaxies: `1296` spectra
    - High-redshift galaxies: `67` systems at `z>2`
  - **Method:**
    - Build a large empirical abundance-calibration sample spanning `12 + log(O/H) = 6.79-9.07`
    - Derive `27` strong-line metallicity calibrations, including `4` newly calibrated diagnostics
    - Quantify intrinsic scatter and validity intervals for each diagnostic
    - Provide parallel calibration families for `t^2 = 0` and `t^2 > 0`
    - Test whether the high-z subset deviates systematically from the local empirical relations
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The combined sample supports a broad empirical metallicity framework with typical intrinsic dispersions of `~0.15-0.35 dex`, which is good enough for practical abundance work but still wide enough that diagnostic choice matters.
    - The `t^2 > 0` solutions shift inferred metallicities upward by roughly `~0.13 dex`, so unresolved temperature fluctuations remain a real systematic in empirical calibrations.
    - The high-z JWST-era sample is broadly consistent with the local-plus-H II-region relations within the current scatter, which means sample diversity matters more than introducing a wholly separate redshift-specific recalibration at this stage.
    - The value of the paper is not only the larger sample size but also the wider validity ranges and four new diagnostics, which should make it easier to choose calibrations matched to sparse or incomplete line sets.
  - **Position:** one of the more reusable methods papers in the recent literature for JWST abundance work, because it directly tests the common assumption that high-z galaxies need a fully separate empirical calibration ladder
