## Executive view

This week links your quasar-CGM interests to the first-galaxy and cosmic-web side of the profile. The strongest direct match is [2603.22657](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.22657) Sanchit Sabhlok, which resolves `Lyalpha`, `He II`, and `C IV` halos around `7` radio-loud quasars at `z~2` and argues for a two-component CGM separated at `~30 kpc`.

The IGM and cosmic-web diagnostics are also better than average. [2603.25788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25788) Christopher Cain introduces a large high-resolution reionization-environment suite with `>200` simulations, while [2603.22108](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.22108) Sharon Lapiner turns FRB scattering into a probe of multiphase post-shock gas in cosmic-web structures and intervening massive halos.

On the first-galaxy side, [2603.23209](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23209) Tae Bong Jeong gives a practical upper limit on how massive a Pop III burst can get even under strong Lyman-Werner suppression, and [2603.24700](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24700) Aidan P. Cloonan gives one of the cleaner morphology-based LRD taxonomies in this period.

## Quasar halos and environment at cosmic noon

- [2603.22657](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.22657) Sanchit Sabhlok `The Two Component Circumgalactic Medium Emission around z~2 Radio-loud Quasars`
  - **Question:** what powers the extended CGM emission around `z~2` radio-loud quasars, and is there evidence for physically distinct inner and outer halo components?
  - **Data:**
    - KCWI `Lyalpha`, `He II`, and `C IV` observations of `7` radio-loud quasars at `z~2`
    - Archival VLA and ALMA radio maps
  - **Method:**
    - Measure the spatial extent and brightness of the line emission
    - Compare the nebular morphology with the radio-jet axis
    - Contrast the inner and outer halo behavior with literature samples
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The quasars show `Lyalpha` emission with diameters of `80-120 kpc` and `He II` plus `C IV` emission on `10-40 kpc` scales.
    - The inner `30 kpc` is brighter in `Lyalpha` by factors of `2-10` than comparable literature samples.
    - `He II` and `C IV` are most naturally explained by quasar photoionization, with the escaping ionizing radiation aligned with the radio jets.
    - The observations support a two-component CGM in which the inner halo at `<30 kpc` is directly affected by the host galaxy, while the outer halo is shaped more by turbulence and the larger environment.
  - **Position:** a directly useful quasar-CGM emission paper because it separates galaxy-dominated and environment-dominated halo regimes around radio-loud quasars

- [2603.23608](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23608) Melanie Kaasinen `Revealing the nature of the starburst galaxies in the z=2.4 overdensity HATLAS J0849`
  - **Question:** what is driving the intense starbursts inside the `z=2.41` overdensity HATLAS J0849, and what do those systems evolve into?
  - **Data:**
    - `0.15 arcsec` CO(4-3), `[C I] 1-0`, and dust-continuum observations
    - Five gas-rich galaxies in the HATLAS J084933.4+021443 overdensity
  - **Method:**
    - Reconstruct cold-gas morphology and kinematics
    - Classify the starbursts as rotating discs, mergers, or barred systems
    - Compare the systems with local early-type galaxies hosting cold gas discs
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Two systems, W and C, are rotating discs with non-axisymmetric radial motions consistent with bars.
    - Of the two most extreme starbursts, W is a lopsided rotation-dominated disc with `v_rot ~ 520 km s^-1`, while T is likely a late-stage merger.
    - At least `>=42%` of gas-rich massive starbursts in overdensities are rotation-dominated discs, which is not yet reproduced systematically by current galaxy-formation models.
    - Beyond `z>1`, discs with `v_rot > 400 km s^-1` are found almost exclusively in overdensities, and these systems already occupy halos comparable to those of the most massive present-day ellipticals in groups and clusters.
  - **Position:** a useful overdensity case study because it shows that extreme starbursts in dense environments are not only mergers; fast rotating discs can dominate there as well

- [2603.24739](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24739) Ciera L. Sargent `A striking excess of red quasars with steep radio spectral slopes: a dusty blow-out phase revealed through AGN-driven shocks?`
  - **Question:** why do red quasars show an excess of steep radio spectra, and does that mark a dusty AGN blow-out phase?
  - **Data:**
    - SDSS quasar sample at `0.2 < z < 2.4`
    - Radio information from FIRST, VLASS, and LoTSS
    - `573` red quasars compared with `1278` blue control quasars
  - **Method:**
    - Classify radio morphologies visually as compact or extended
    - Compare the radio spectral slopes and detection fractions of red and blue quasar samples
    - Test whether the trends strengthen with dust extinction
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Red quasars with compact radio morphologies show an excess of steep-spectrum radio emission with `alpha_1.4-3GHz ~ -1`, unlike blue quasars or red quasars with extended radio structure.
    - The strength of the steep-spectrum excess increases with dust extinction, as does the overall radio-detection fraction.
    - For the dustiest systems with `E(B-V) > 0.4`, most steep-spectrum objects have radio luminosities consistent with wind-shock models having efficiencies up to `7%`.
    - The favored interpretation is a dusty blow-out phase where compact jets or AGN-driven winds shock the dusty nuclear ISM.
  - **Position:** a useful quasar-evolution paper because it ties dust reddening, compact radio emission, and AGN-driven shock signatures into one evolutionary stage

## IGM and cosmic-web diagnostics

- [2603.25788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25788) Christopher Cain `Introducing SAGUARO -- Simulating IGM Evolution and Environments At High Resolution: Setup and First Results`
  - **Question:** what changes when reionization-era IGM evolution is simulated at high resolution across a broad grid of large-scale environments?
  - **Data:**
    - SAGUARO suite of `>200` coupled radiation-hydrodynamic simulations
    - Box sizes of `2` and `0.25 h^-1 Mpc`
    - Grids spanning photoionization rate, reionization redshift, and box-scale density
  - **Method:**
    - Resolve the cold neutral-universe Jeans scale while still reaching box sizes that capture halos above the atomic-cooling limit
    - Vary additional physics such as X-ray pre-heating, recombination radiation, baryon-dark-matter streaming, and alternative dark-matter models
    - Measure thermal structure, opacity, self-shielding, halo statistics, and Lyalpha transmission
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The suite is designed explicitly to capture the small-scale gas dynamics that shape the post-reionization IGM and high-z observables such as the Lyalpha forest.
    - It spans the realistic environmental parameter space rather than studying a single representative reionization history.
    - The first results already quantify the IGM thermal structure, opacity, self-shielding, photon-budget impact, halo mass function response, and Lyalpha transmission behavior across that parameter space.
  - **Position:** a valuable IGM infrastructure paper because it fills the gap between idealized small-box studies and large low-resolution reionization simulations

- [2603.22108](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.22108) Sharon Lapiner `Probing the Cosmic Web with Fast Radio Bursts. I. Scattering`
  - **Question:** can FRB scattering measure multiphase post-shock gas in cosmic sheets, filaments, and halo outskirts?
  - **Data:**
    - Theoretical models of FRB propagation through sheets, filaments, and the CGM of halos
    - Multiphase post-accretion-shock gas with `~10^4 K` cloudlets in pressure equilibrium
  - **Method:**
    - Model the small-scale density inhomogeneities created by thermal instabilities in cosmic-web objects
    - Predict the resulting FRB scattering along average and rare sightlines
    - Translate the lack or presence of redshift trends into constraints on volume filling fraction, turbulence, and cloud properties
  - **Conclusion:**
    - High-redshift sheets and filaments contribute negligibly to the total scattering along typical sightlines, though rare hot-filament intersections may still be detectable.
    - If turbulent cloudlets in intervening massive halos have volume-filling fraction `f_v >= 10^-3`, they should produce substantial cumulative scattering and a clear correlation between scattering time and source redshift.
    - The lack of such a correlation in current data implies either strong non-thermal pressure support in the cool CGM or damping of the small-scale density fluctuations.
    - Future localized FRB samples can turn these effects into quantitative bounds on cloud densities, sizes, turbulence, and filling fractions.
  - **Position:** a useful cosmic-web and CGM diagnostic paper because it shows where FRB scattering should and should not be sensitive to multiphase gas

- [2603.26352](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.26352) Jongwon Park `Population III star formation in an X-ray background: IV. On-the-fly calculation of radiation backgrounds and their impact on the intergalactic medium`
  - **Question:** how does an on-the-fly X-ray background from Pop III supernovae alter IGM heating and subsequent Pop III formation?
  - **Data:**
    - Cosmological zoom-in simulations of regions with different mean densities
    - On-the-fly global X-ray and Lyman-Werner backgrounds generated by Pop III supernovae
  - **Method:**
    - Compute the radiation backgrounds during the simulation rather than in post-processing
    - Track the feedback loop between Pop III stars and the backgrounds they generate
    - Compare the response of the IGM and Pop III number density in different environments
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Pop III supernovae generate a weak X-ray background of `J_X,21 ~ 10^-5` and a moderate LW background of `J_LW,21 ~ 10^-1`, with the latter rising to `~10^1-10^2` below `z~12` once Pop II star formation begins.
    - The net X-ray feedback is positive and increases the Pop III number density, especially in underdense regions.
    - This enhancement is stronger when the X-ray background is computed on-the-fly rather than applied in post-processing, which shows that the feedback loop matters.
    - The additional X-ray heating raises the high-z Thomson optical depth while still remaining consistent with Planck 2018 constraints on the total optical depth.
  - **Position:** a useful Pop III feedback paper because it quantifies how self-consistent X-ray backgrounds reshape the IGM into which later first galaxies form

## First galaxies and morphology

- [2603.23209](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23209) Tae Bong Jeong `How Massive Can a Population III Starburst Be? Simulating the First Galaxies with High Lyman-Werner Background`
  - **Question:** how massive can a Pop III starburst become in atomic-cooling halos under strong Lyman-Werner suppression?
  - **Data:**
    - Cosmological radiation-hydrodynamic zoom-in simulations of first galaxies
    - Atomic-cooling halos with `M_vir ~ 10^8 Msun` at `7 < z < 8`
    - Lyman-Werner backgrounds up to `J_LW <= 10^4 J_21`
  - **Method:**
    - Vary the Lyman-Werner background and the star-formation efficiency, up to `epsilon_ff = 1.0`
    - Track how delayed cooling affects the timing and mass of the resulting Pop III starburst
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Observable Pop III starbursts can occur when the Lyman-Werner field is sufficiently high, `>=10^3 J_21`, because the background delays star formation until a larger burst can occur.
    - Even in those favorable cases, the Pop III burst mass stays below `10^6 Msun`.
    - Internal metal enrichment after the first Pop III supernovae still terminates the pristine phase quickly.
    - The paper therefore argues that observable Pop III starbursts should be rare but detectable in large lensed surveys such as GLIMPSE and future programs like VENUS.
  - **Position:** a useful upper-limit paper for Pop III observability, because it quantifies how far the burst mass can be pushed before self-enrichment shuts the window

- [2603.24700](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24700) Aidan P. Cloonan `A PANORAMIC of UV-optical morphologies of "Little Red Dots": Two groups of LRDs distinguished by UV half-light radius`
  - **Question:** do LRD morphologies support a single family, or at least two distinct sub-populations separated by UV size?
  - **Data:**
    - PANORAMIC pure-parallel photometric LRD sample
    - UV-to-optical morphology measurements plus a spectroscopic comparison sample
  - **Method:**
    - Measure sizes with single-Sersic fits and joint PSF+Sersic modeling
    - Track half-light radius across the Balmer break
    - Stack resolved and unresolved subsamples separately
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Rest-UV emission is typically more extended than rest-optical emission in LRDs.
    - Across the Balmer break, the rest-optical sizes become extremely compact, with `R_50,opt <= 100 pc`.
    - One LRD group stays compact across the full UV-optical range, while the other has extended UV emission with `R_50,UV > 200 pc`.
    - The dichotomy is consistent with UV light arising from different mixtures of starlight plus dense dust-poor gas surrounding an AGN, potentially linked to different black-hole seed masses.
  - **Position:** a strong morphology-driven LRD paper because it turns the class into two empirically separable groups rather than one blended category
