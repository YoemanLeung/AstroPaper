## Executive view

This week is stronger in reionization diagnostics and JWST interpretation than in direct structure-mapping papers. The cleanest links to your core interests are [2602.17499](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17499) Giordano Felicioni, [2602.15803](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15803) Anirban Chakraborty, [2602.17329](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17329) I. Jermann, and [2602.17593](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17593) Sayak Dutta: faint lensed `z>7.5` UV slopes, higher-order `21 cm`-galaxy statistics at `z=7`, MIRI-red source selection in the deepest MIRI field, and one directly reusable CGM anisotropy measurement.

The high-z interpretation papers are also useful because they test competing physical pictures rather than just adding more candidates. [2602.15935](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15935) John Chisholm gives a non-AGN LRD scenario tied to forming globular clusters, while [2602.18556](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18556) Laura Sommovigo shows that UV-bright `z>10` systems and dusty `z~7` descendants can coexist if the ISM is porous and turbulent.

[2602.18068](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18068) A. Calabrò is the main morphology anchor this week. It puts numerical constraints on merger fractions, merger rates, and SFR enhancement at `5<z<14`, which makes it more useful than the usual qualitative early-assembly discussion.

## Reionization and first-light diagnostics

- [2602.17499](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17499) Giordano Felicioni `UV slopes of Starforming Galaxies in Strong Lensing fields at the Epoch of Reionization with JWST`
  - **Question:** how blue are faint strongly lensed galaxies at `z>7.5`, and what do their UV slopes imply for dust buildup and ionizing-photon escape during reionization?
  - **Data:**
    - JWST/NIRCam plus NIRSpec: CANUCS, Technicolor, JUMPS, Silver Bullet, UNCOVER, and MEGASCIENCE data across `7` strong-lensing cluster fields
    - Galaxy sample: `378` galaxies at `7.5<z<12.5`, including `45` with spectra, down to `M_UV~ -16`
  - **Method:**
    - Measure `beta` with a forward-modeling procedure suited to very faint lensed sources
    - Compare `beta` as a function of redshift and UV luminosity
    - Estimate `f_esc` for a `14`-galaxy subsample using `beta`, galaxy size, and `Hbeta` equivalent width calibrated at low redshift
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The sample has average values `beta = -2.3 +- 0.4`, `z = 8.5 +- 1.0`, and `M_UV = -18 +- 1`, so the faint lensed population remains very blue.
    - There is no significant `beta-z` evolution across `7.5<z<12.5`, which supports a flattened UV-slope trend above `z~7.5`.
    - The `beta-M_UV` trend is only weakly negative, which is consistent with similarly low dust content across the low- and high-mass systems in this regime.
    - The inferred escape fraction for the emission-line subsample is high, `f_esc = 0.26 +- 0.22`, although the low-z calibration likely overestimates the true value.
  - **Position:** a strong observational input for faint-galaxy reionization work, especially where UV slope is used as a proxy for low dust and elevated ionizing escape

- [2602.15803](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15803) Anirban Chakraborty `Nearest Neighbour-Based Statistics for 21cm-Galaxy Cross-Correlations in the Epoch of Reionization`
  - **Question:** can nearest-neighbour statistics recover `21 cm`-galaxy cross-information more robustly than the standard two-point function in the noisy, foreground-filtered EoR regime?
  - **Data:**
    - Self-consistent mock `21 cm` fields
    - Simulated catalog of line-emitting galaxies at `z=7`
    - Instrumental-noise and aggressive-foreground-filtering realizations for the cross-correlation tests
  - **Method:**
    - Measure `k`-nearest-neighbour cumulative distribution functions instead of only two-point statistics
    - Compare the `kNN CDF` performance directly against the standard cross-correlation estimator
    - Test both methods at fixed global ionized fraction across different reionization morphologies
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The `kNN CDF` statistic detects `21 cm`-galaxy cross-correlations more effectively than the two-point function, even after instrumental noise and aggressive foreground filtering are added.
    - At fixed global ionized fraction, the higher-order statistic still separates reionization models that remain degenerate in the two-point approach.
    - The gain comes from retaining non-Gaussian information from the joint galaxy-neutral-gas distribution rather than truncating the problem at second order.
  - **Position:** a useful methods paper for `21 cm` plus galaxy-survey synergies, especially if future EoR analyses need information beyond standard two-point estimators

- [2602.17304](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17304) Sukhdeep Singh Gill `The Epoch of Reionization 21 cm Bispectrum at z=8.2 from MWA data II: Smooth Component Filtering`
  - **Question:** can the `21 cm` bispectrum at `z=8.2` be constrained from MWA data after smooth-component foreground filtering?
  - **Data:**
    - MWA observations at `154.2 MHz`, corresponding to `z=8.2`
    - Simulated `21 cm` signal cubes used to validate the bispectrum pipeline
  - **Method:**
    - Apply smooth-component filtering to suppress spectrally smooth foregrounds
    - Validate recovery of the input bispectrum for modes with `k_parallel >= 0.135 Mpc^-1`
    - Measure the cylindrical and spherical bispectrum in the cleaned EoR window
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Smooth-component filtering suppresses the cylindrical bispectrum foreground amplitude by `3-4` orders of magnitude and also reduces artifacts from missing frequency channels.
    - The cleaned window is substantially better at small `k_perp`, enabling a spherical-bispectrum constraint in the region `(k_1_perp, k_2_perp, k_3_perp) <= 0.026 Mpc^-1` and `(k_1_parallel, k_2_parallel, k_3_parallel) > 0.135 Mpc^-1`.
    - The most stringent limits at `k_1 = 0.281 Mpc^-1` are `Delta^3_LL = -(1.25 x 10^4)^3 mK^3` and `Delta^3_UL = (1.22 x 10^4)^3 mK^3`, still consistent with system noise rather than a detection.
  - **Position:** a useful pipeline paper for bispectrum-based EoR inference, with immediate relevance if higher-order `21 cm` statistics become observationally practical

## Extreme high-z galaxies and interpretation

- [2602.17329](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17329) I. Jermann `MIDIS: The identification of deep MIRI-red sources as candidates for extreme Balmer-break and line emitting galaxies at high-z`
  - **Question:** what are the deepest MIRI-red sources in MIDIS, and are they better explained as extreme line emitters, strong Balmer-break systems, or rare LRD-like objects?
  - **Data:**
    - JWST/MIRI Deep Imaging Survey in the HUDF over `2.4 arcmin^2`
    - Ultra-deep `F560W` plus ancillary JWST/HST photometry, including `F444W` and dropout information
    - Candidate sample: `7` MIRI-red galaxies, including one `F115W` dropout and one extreme `F560W` source undetected in `F444W`
  - **Method:**
    - Define MIRI-red sources as `5 sigma` `F560W` detections with `m_F444W - m_F560W >= 0.5`
    - Empirically estimate detection purity and completeness at the faint survey limit
    - Interpret the red colors using equivalent-width and Balmer-break thresholds for line-emitter and evolved-population scenarios
  - **Conclusion:**
    - A `5 sigma` detection at `28.75` mag has purity `92%` and completeness `54%`, so the survey is deep enough to find a very rare red population while still quantifying selection losses.
    - The observed colors are consistent with line-emitter solutions of `EW_0(Halpha) >= 750 Angstrom` or `EW_0(Hbeta+[OIII]) >= 600 Angstrom`, or with Balmer breaks of at least `1.6`.
    - The most extreme source can be explained by `EW_0(Halpha) ~ 6000 Angstrom`, `EW_0(Hbeta+[OIII]) ~ 4000 Angstrom`, or an LRD-like Balmer break of `6.3`.
    - There are fewer MIRI-red sources than expected from simple extrapolations of `Halpha` or `Hbeta+[OIII]` luminosity functions, and no `z>10` LRD candidates appear in the MIDIS volume.
  - **Position:** a strong selection-and-interpretation paper for extreme MIRI-red populations, with immediate value for Balmer-break versus line-emitter triage in deep JWST fields

- [2602.15935](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15935) John Chisholm `Little Red Dots as Globular Clusters in Formation`
  - **Question:** do Little Red Dots require accreting black holes, or can at least part of the population be explained as globular clusters in formation?
  - **Data:**
    - Observed LRD luminosity functions at `z~5-7`
    - Local globular-cluster mass-function constraints used as the evolutionary endpoint
  - **Method:**
    - Model rest-frame UV light from a very young stellar population and rest-frame optical light from a short-lived supermassive star with `M > 10^4 Msun`
    - Evolve the observed LRD luminosity function forward using standard evolutionary and mass-loss prescriptions
    - Compare the evolved population with the present-day globular-cluster mass function and number density
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The broad spectral profiles of LRDs are consistent with this stellar interpretation, although the temperatures and bolometric luminosities still favor reprocessing by optically thick winds not yet captured in the models.
    - The evolved luminosity function produces a present-day mass function with turnover at `log10(M_*/Msun) = 5.3` plus a high-mass exponential cutoff, consistent with local globular-cluster populations.
    - The implied present-day number density is `~0.3 Mpc^-3`, close to the observed local globular-cluster density.
    - If this picture is correct, LRD descendants should show multiple-population abundance signatures such as enhanced He and N plus possible Na-O and Al-Mg anticorrelations.
  - **Position:** a concrete non-AGN alternative for LRDs, useful mainly because it makes falsifiable links between JWST LRDs and present-day globular-cluster archaeology

- [2602.18556](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18556) Laura Sommovigo `Blue Monsters and Dusty Descendants: Reconciling UV and IR Emission from Galaxies from z=7, up to z= 14`
  - **Question:** how can UV-bright, apparently dust-poor galaxies at `z>10` coexist with dusty IR-luminous systems of similar mass by `z~7`?
  - **Data:**
    - JWST UV luminosity functions at `z~14-7`
    - ALMA IR luminosity functions at `z~7`
    - Minimal galaxy-formation framework with dust production tied to Type II supernovae
  - **Method:**
    - Fit both UV and IR luminosity functions with two free parameters: star-formation efficiency `epsilon_*` and SN dust yield `y_d`
    - Compare a uniform ISM with a porous turbulent ISM that opens low-column escape channels
    - Test whether large-grain dust distributions can reduce the UV-IR tension
  - **Conclusion:**
    - In a uniform ISM, the bright-end UV and IR luminosity functions are inconsistent with a single dust model: the UV data prefer `y_d <= 0.01 Msun`, while the `z=7` IR data need `y_d ~ 0.1 Msun` unless `epsilon_*` rises above `5-10%`.
    - A porous turbulent ISM resolves most of this tension by boosting UV escape along low-column sightlines while leaving the total absorbed energy, and thus the IR luminosity, nearly unchanged.
    - Large-grain dust distributions matter less once ISM porosity and radiative-transfer flattening are included.
    - At `z>10`, even strong turbulence cannot reproduce the bright UV end if the dust yield is already high, so the early systems likely need either efficient dust removal or substantial ISM dust growth only by `z~7`.
  - **Position:** a useful dust-physics framework for connecting very blue early JWST galaxies to dusty descendants a few hundred Myr later

## CGM and early assembly context

- [2602.17593](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17593) Sayak Dutta `MUSEQuBES: Probing Anisotropies in Gas and Metal Distributions in the Circumgalactic Medium`
  - **Question:** is the CGM azimuthally isotropic once both `H I` and `O VI` are measured around isolated galaxies across a broad stellar-mass range?
  - **Data:**
    - `113` isolated galaxies at `0.12<z<0.75`
    - `91` new measurements from MUSEQuBES
    - `H I` and `O VI` absorber samples evaluated within the virial radius
  - **Method:**
    - Measure covering fractions as a function of azimuthal angle relative to the projected disk
    - Separate low-mass and high-mass host galaxies
    - Use halo-circular-velocity-normalized two-point correlation functions to compare absorber kinematics along major and minor axes
  - **Conclusion:**
    - Low-mass galaxies with `7 < log10(M_*/Msun) < 9` show enhanced `H I` covering fraction along both the disk plane and the polar direction for `log10 N(HI) >= 14.5`.
    - The `O VI` covering fraction for `log10 N(OVI) >= 14.0` also shows a tentative bimodal enhancement along both projected major and minor axes in the low-mass halos.
    - High-mass galaxies with `9 < log10(M_*/Msun) < 11.3` do not show the same strong azimuthal dependence, especially in `O VI`.
    - `O VI` absorbers are kinematically narrower along the disk plane than along polar directions, so the spatial distribution can look isotropic while the velocity structure still retains memory of the gas origin.
  - **Position:** a directly useful CGM geometry paper for interpreting anisotropic inflow and outflow signatures in absorber-selected sightlines

- [2602.18068](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18068) A. Calabrò `Investigating the role of mergers in galaxy assembly in the early Universe (z > 5)`
  - **Question:** how strongly do mergers shape galaxy growth, star formation, and morphology at `5<z<14`?
  - **Data:**
    - `1233` galaxies at `5<z<14` across `6` extragalactic fields
    - JWST imaging with `S/N-pixel > 3` in the rest-frame optical `F444W`
    - HST plus JWST photometry used for non-parametric SED modeling
  - **Method:**
    - Identify disturbed morphologies with Gini, `M_20`, and Asymmetry criteria in `F444W`
    - Convert merger fractions to merger rates using tracer-dependent observability timescales
    - Compare SFRs and stellar-mass growth of mergers with redshift- and mass-matched non-mergers
  - **Conclusion:**
    - The major-merger fraction is `~5%`, while broader major-plus-minor tracers raise it to `~13%`, with no strong evolution in the raw fraction from `z=0` to `z~8`.
    - After accounting for the shorter observability times at high redshift, the merger rate rises by more than `1 dex` from `z=1` to `z=7`, reaching `~2 mergers galaxy^-1 Gyr^-1` at `5<z<10` for major mergers and roughly three times higher for major-plus-minor mergers.
    - Over `10 Myr`, mergers have SFRs about `1.7` times higher than matched non-mergers, which supports short-lived merger-triggered bursts.
    - Even so, mergers contribute only `5-10%` of the total mass build-up across the redshift range, so they are important but not dominant for early stellar-mass assembly.
  - **Position:** a useful calibration paper for interpreting disturbed high-z morphologies, because it separates merger visibility, merger rate, burst enhancement, and actual mass-growth contribution
